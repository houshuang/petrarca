Petrarca

An intelligent read-later app for the curious mind

✦ Overview

Petrarca is a knowledge-aware read-later app. It ingests articles from your Twitter bookmarks, Readwise saves, a Chrome extension, and email forwarding, then processes each one with AI to extract claims, topics, and summaries. Over time it builds a model of what you already know and what you find interesting, so it can surface genuinely novel content and dim the familiar.

Named after Francesco Petrarca (Petrarch), the Renaissance humanist who pioneered systematic methods for reading, annotating, and synthesizing knowledge from texts.

AI-Extracted Claims

Every article is decomposed into 10–30 atomic claims, typed and embedded for semantic comparison.

Knowledge Tracking

The app remembers what you've read. Familiar material dims; novel content stands out.

Interest Model

Your reading signals shape a personal interest model that ranks articles by relevance and novelty.

Delta Reports

Per-topic AI syntheses show you what's new across all the articles in a given topic.

✦ Getting Started

Petrarca runs as a mobile-first app (Expo/React Native) and is also accessible as a web app. Content is fetched and processed on a server every four hours, then served via nginx. The app syncs automatically on launch.

Web App
alifstian.duckdns.org:8084
Expo (native)
exp://alifstian.duckdns.org:8082
Content Server
Port 8083, nginx, 4-hour refresh cycle
Pipeline
Gemini Flash for extraction, Gemini embeddings for claims

On first launch the app downloads all articles and the knowledge index from the server. After that, it caches everything locally and checks for updates in the background each time you open it.

✦ Capturing Articles

There are four ways to get articles into Petrarca. All of them feed into the same AI processing pipeline.

Twitter / X Bookmarks

Bookmark any tweet that contains an article URL. The server fetches your bookmarks every four hours via twikit, reconstructs threads for self-threads, extracts the linked articles, and processes them with Gemini Flash.

Lag: up to 4 hours. Twitter cookies expire periodically and may need re-authentication.

Readwise Reader

Save articles through Readwise's browser extension, share sheet, or RSS subscriptions. Articles with reading progress in Readwise get priority. Same four-hour sync cycle as Twitter bookmarks.

Chrome Extension (Web Clipper)

A lightweight Chrome extension lives in the clipper/ directory. Click the floating ✦ button in the bottom-right corner of any page, or use the keyboard shortcut Cmd+Shift+S. The clipper captures the URL, page content, and any selected text, then sends it to the server immediately. You can see detected topic tags and add a note before saving.

Install: chrome://extensions → Developer Mode → Load unpacked → select the clipper/ folder.

Email Forwarding

Forward newsletters or article links to the configured email address. A Cloudflare Email Worker receives the message, extracts article URLs (filtering out tracking and image links), and processes them through the pipeline. Particularly useful for newsletters, which often have a "view in browser" link the worker can follow. Handles up to three URLs per email.

✦ The AI Pipeline

Every article passes through a multi-stage processing pipeline on the server. Here is what happens to each piece of content.

  1. Fetch — Content is retrieved using a three-tier strategy: trafilatura first, then requests with browser headers, then lxml as a fallback. Markdown is cleaned of nav menus, cookie banners, and subscribe cruft.
  2. Extract — Gemini Flash reads the full text and produces: title, author, one-line summary, full summary, sections, key claims, broad and specific topics, entity topics, novelty claims, and content type.
  3. Decompose — The article is broken into 10–30 atomic claims, each typed as factual, procedural, evaluative, relational, or temporal. Every claim is self-contained and never starts with a pronoun.
  4. Embed — All claims receive Gemini embeddings. Cosine similarity between claims is computed to build a knowledge index: paragraph-level novelty mappings, a cross-article novelty matrix, and per-topic delta reports.
  5. Serve — Processed articles and the knowledge index are written to JSON and served via nginx on port 8083. The app syncs on launch.

✦ Feed Tab

The Feed is the main landing screen. Articles are ranked by a composite score drawn from your personal interest model.

Interest Match
40% — how well topics align with what you read and engage with
Freshness
25% — newer articles score higher
Discovery Bonus
20% — topics you haven't explored yet get a boost
Variety
15% — avoids topic clustering in consecutive entries

After you have read several articles, a curiosity scoring layer re-ranks the feed: articles with roughly 70% novel claims score highest, balancing learning with familiarity.

✦ Feed elements

Topic filter chips scroll horizontally at the top, letting you narrow the feed to a single topic. Each article row shows the title, a one-line summary, a novelty claim preview, hostname and reading time, topic tags, and a "N new claims" hint. In-progress articles appear in a Continue Reading section at the top (maximum two). Articles you have already read appear at the bottom of the feed, dimmed.

✦ Topics Tab

Articles grouped by broad topic, with duplicates merged and formatting normalized. Each topic row shows the article count and, when the knowledge index is loaded, a knowledge progress bar — green for new claims, gray for known.

Tap a topic to expand it and see two things: a delta report ("What's new in [Topic]"), which is an AI-generated synthesis of novel information across all articles in that topic along with the top three novel claims; and the article list for that topic, where tapping any article opens the reader.

✦ Queue Tab

The Queue holds articles you have explicitly saved for later by swiping right in the Feed. It is a simple, personal reading list. Swipe right on a queued article to remove it. Tap to open the reader. When empty, the screen explains how to add articles.

✦ The Reader

Tapping any article opens the reader, which renders the full article text from cleaned markdown. A header shows the title, author, hostname, estimated reading time, and topic tags. Scroll position is saved and restored between sessions, so you can pick up exactly where you left off.

✦ Reading modes

When the knowledge system has enough data, a mode toggle appears with three options:

Full

All content at normal opacity. The default reading experience.

Guided

Familiar paragraphs are dimmed to 55% opacity. Novel content stays vivid.

New Only

Familiar sections collapse into "tap to expand" bars. Only novel material is visible.

✦ Claims card

A "What's new for you" card shows each article's claims classified against your knowledge ledger. Claims appear in green (NEW), gray (KNOWN), or an intermediate state (EXTENDS). Pressing Done at the end of an article marks all its claims as encountered in the knowledge system.

✦ Knowledge System

Petrarca builds a personal knowledge model that tracks which claims you have encountered and how well you remember them. This happens automatically in the background — you do not need to do anything special.

✦ How it works

The server pre-computes claim similarities, paragraph-level novelty mappings, and per-topic delta reports. On the client side, a lightweight FSRS-inspired decay model tracks each claim:

  • Reading an article gives its claims a 30-day stability window.
  • Highlighting a paragraph doubles stability to 60 days.
  • Re-reading reinforces: stability is multiplied by 2.5.
  • Claims decay over time. If you haven't encountered a claim in months, the system eventually treats it as unknown again.

When you open the next article on a related topic, the system finds matching claims via embedding similarity and classifies them as NEW, KNOWN, or EXTENDS. This drives the paragraph dimming in Guided and New Only modes, the claims card in the reader, and the curiosity-based re-ranking in the feed.

✦ Interest model

Separate from knowledge tracking, the interest model learns what topics you care about. Every interaction contributes a weighted signal: swiping to keep an article, opening it, finishing it, highlighting passages, and tapping topic chips all shape your profile. Signals decay over 30 days with Bayesian smoothing, so your interests can evolve naturally. The feed ranking formula uses this model as its primary input.

✦ Gestures & Interactions

  • Tap Open an article in the reader. Navigate between tabs, topics, and modes.
  • Swipe Right In the Feed, adds an article to your Queue. In the Queue, removes it.
  • Swipe Left In the Feed, dismisses an article from view.
  • Long Press In the reader, highlights a paragraph with a warm amber left border and haptic feedback on mobile.
  • Pull Down Refreshes content. A rotating ✦ ornament serves as the loading indicator.
  • Done At the bottom of the reader, marks the article as read and records all its claims in the knowledge ledger.

✦ Content Sync

On launch the app checks a manifest hash against the cached version. If it is the first launch, a blocking download fetches all articles and the knowledge index from the server. On subsequent launches the app loads from cache immediately and checks for updates in the background. New articles and an updated knowledge index are synced automatically whenever the server pipeline completes a fresh run.

The server pipeline runs every four hours. After bookmarking a tweet or saving a Readwise article, expect your content to appear in the app within that window. The Chrome extension and email forwarding trigger processing immediately, but the article still needs to pass through the full AI pipeline before it shows up in your feed.
