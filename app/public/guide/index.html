Petrarca

How to actually use this thing — flows, integrations, and what everything does

✦ The Big Picture

Petrarca is a read-later system that models what you know. Content flows in from multiple sources (Twitter bookmarks, Readwise Reader, Chrome web clipper, email forwarding, link ingest from the reader, topic research), gets processed by Gemini Flash on a server every 4 hours, and arrives in the app as structured articles with summaries, atomic claims, interest topics, novelty scores, entity annotations, and follow-up research questions.

As you read, the system automatically tracks what you've encountered and uses FSRS-based memory decay to determine which claims are still “known” vs forgotten — shaping what gets highlighted as new in future articles.

Capture (bookmark / clip / email / link ingest / topic research) → Pipeline processes (every 4h) → Content appears in app → You browse, swipe, read → Knowledge model updates automatically → Familiar paragraphs dim → Feed re-ranks based on novelty and interest.

AI-Extracted Claims

Every article is decomposed into 10–30 atomic claims, typed and embedded for semantic comparison.

Knowledge Tracking

The app remembers what you've read. Familiar material dims; novel content stands out.

Interest Model

Your reading signals shape a personal interest model that ranks articles by relevance and novelty.

Entity Deep-Dive

People, books, concepts, and places are annotated with dotted underlines. Long-press to explore.

Cross-Article Connections

Claims shared between articles surface as inline “Also in” links and a connected reading section.

Voice & AI Chat

Record voice notes while reading; ask the AI follow-up questions with full article context.

✦ Getting Content In

You have seven ways to feed Petrarca. They all end at the same place: import_url.py on the server, which fetches the article, extracts text, runs it through Gemini Flash for structuring (summary, claims, sections, topics, entities, follow-up questions), and adds it to the content pool.

Twitter / X Bookmarks

Bookmark any tweet that contains an article URL. The server fetches your bookmarks every four hours via twikit, reconstructs threads for self-threads, extracts the linked articles, and processes them with Gemini Flash.

Lag: up to 4 hours. Twitter cookies expire periodically and may need re-authentication.

Readwise Reader

Save articles through Readwise's browser extension, share sheet, or RSS subscriptions. The same four-hour cron fetches your Readwise items via their API and processes them identically to Twitter bookmarks.

Why both? Twitter catches spontaneous discoveries. Readwise catches deliberate saves and RSS reading.

Chrome Extension (Web Clipper)

Click the floating ✦ button in the bottom-right corner of any page, or use Cmd+Shift+S. The clipper captures the URL, page content, and any selected text, then sends it to the server immediately. You can see detected topic tags and add a note before saving.

Install: chrome://extensions → Developer Mode → Load unpacked → select the clipper/ folder.

Email Forwarding

Forward newsletters or article links to the configured email address. A Cloudflare Email Worker receives the message, extracts article URLs (filtering out tracking and image links), and processes them. Handles up to three URLs per email.

Manual URL Import

From a terminal: ssh alif then cd /opt/petrarca && .venv/bin/python3 scripts/import_url.py "URL" --tag manual

Developer-only. Immediate processing.

Link Auto-Ingest from Reader

While reading an article, tap any link in the text. The URL is sent to the server's /ingest endpoint and processed in the background. An inline badge shows the ingest status. The article will appear in your feed after processing — no need to leave the app or copy-paste URLs.

Topic Research

On the Topics tab, expand any topic and tap ↗ Find more on [Topic]. Gemini search grounding finds real articles on that topic from the open web — not hallucinated URLs, but actual Google-discovered pages. The top 3 results are auto-ingested into your pipeline.

✦ Navigation

The Feed is the single home screen. There is no visible tab bar — all secondary screens are accessed through the ✦ drawer.

✦ The ✦ Drawer

Tap the ✦ button in the feed header to open the drawer — a dark bottom sheet providing access to all secondary screens:

  • Voice Notes — browse recorded voice notes with transcripts and action chips
  • Activity Log — timeline of reading, system, and research events
  • Your Landscape — topic bubble map, reading stats, cross-thread bridges
  • Reading Trails — follow threads of connected ideas through your library
  • Queue — your explicit reading list
  • User Guide — this guide

The drawer also has two quick actions: Triage (card-by-card article decisions) and Voice Note (record a thought).

✦ The Feed

The feed is the main landing screen. On launch, the app checks the server for new content by comparing a manifest hash. If it changed, it downloads updates and merges with your existing state. Your reading progress and signals are stored locally and never lost.

✦ Feed Sections

  • Up Next — your current in-progress article (with progress bar), or the next queued article, or an algorithmic recommendation
  • Recommended — a hero card showing the top-ranked article with novelty claim preview
  • Topic Pills — horizontal scrollable topic filters
  • Lens Tabs — sticky tab bar for switching between views

✦ Lens Tabs

Four ways to view your articles, switchable via the sticky lens tabs:

Latest
Chronological, newest first, with ingest source and relative time
Best
Ranked by blended score: interest model (60%) + knowledge curiosity (40%)
Topics
Articles grouped by topic cluster
Quick
Short articles (< 5 minutes), sorted by novelty

✦ Feed Interactions

Mobile:

  • Swipe right → Queue (adds to your reading queue)
  • Swipe left → Dismiss (hides from feed)
  • Pull down → Refresh (✦ ornament rotates)
  • Tap → Open in reader

Web (desktop):

  • Hover → Shows ✓ (archive) and ✕ (dismiss) action buttons

  • Keyboard: j/k navigate, Enter open, 1–4 switch lens

On web, articles display in a 2-column grid (max-width 1100px).

✦ Other Screens

✦ Topics

Articles grouped by primary topic. Each topic is a collapsible cluster with article count and reading state dots (grey = unread, rubric = reading, green = read). When the knowledge engine is ready, topics show delta reports — a summary of what's new. Each expanded topic has a “↗ Find more” button for topic research. On web, a master-detail layout shows the topic list on the left and expanded cluster detail on the right.

✦ Queue

Your explicit reading list. Articles queued from the feed (swipe right) or from the reader (“+ Queue” buttons) appear here in order. Mobile: swipe to remove. Web: hover to reveal ✕ remove button. Persisted to local storage.

✦ Activity Log

A vertical timeline of everything happening in the system. Colored dots per event type, ✦ markers for interest signals, day separators. Filter toggles: All / Reading / System / Research.

✦ Your Landscape

A birds-eye view of your reading territory: topic bubble map (sized by article count, colored by trend), reading stats (articles explored, topics touched, hours this week), and cross-thread bridges showing where topics overlap.

✦ Reading Trails

Follow threads of connected ideas: active threads with article sequences, thread detail with read/unread status, and bridges between threads. On web, a master-detail layout shows threads on the left and expanded detail on the right.

✦ Voice Notes

Date-grouped list of all voice notes with transcripts and extracted action chips (research/tag/remember). Each note shows when it was recorded, what article it was attached to, and the LLM-identified actions.

✦ Floating Feedback Capture

A floating ✦ button appears on every screen (bottom-right). Tap to record a voice note (mobile) or type text feedback, with auto-detected context (current screen, article). Long-press to hide for the session.

✦ The Reader

Tap any article to open the reader. The reader presents the full article with intelligent paragraph dimming based on the knowledge engine's assessment of what you already know, entity annotations, cross-article connections, and follow-up research prompts.

✦ Reader Structure

  • Header: Title, author, source, date, ☆ bookmark toggle, and … menu
  • Summary: One-paragraph overview
  • Mode toggle: Full / Guided / New Only
  • Article body: Full markdown-rendered content with paragraph-level dimming and entity underlines
  • Highlighting: Long-press any paragraph to highlight it (amber border, haptic feedback on mobile)
  • Claims card: Atomic claims extracted from the article
  • ✦ FURTHER INQUIRY: Follow-up research questions generated by the pipeline
  • ✦ CONNECTED READING: Cross-article connections based on shared claims
  • Related articles: Same Topic / Shared Concepts / Same Source
  • Footer bar: Done button + “UP NEXT” showing next queued article title
  • Post-read interest card: After tapping “Done”, hierarchical topic feedback

✦ Reading Modes

A mode toggle at the top of the reader lets you switch between three modes:

Full

All content at normal opacity. No filtering. The default reading experience.

Guided

Familiar paragraphs dimmed. Novel content stays vivid. Green left border on novel paragraphs draws the eye.

New Only

Familiar paragraphs collapsed into “skip” indicators. Only novel sections expanded. Green left border on novel content.

The dimming is automatic — it comes from the knowledge engine comparing the article's atomic claims against claims you've previously encountered, using embedding similarity and FSRS-based memory decay.

✦ Novel Paragraph Markers

In Guided and New Only modes, novel and mostly-novel paragraphs get a 2px green left border to draw the eye. This is the primary visual cue for “this is new to you.”

✦ ☆ Bookmark and … Menu

☆ Bookmark: Tap to toggle bookmark status. Persisted across sessions.

… Menu opens a dropdown with: Article info (metadata, word count, source), Open source (original URL), Ask AI (bottom-sheet chat), Voice note (record a voice memo), and Research topic (background research).

✦ Claims Card

A “What's new for you” card shows each article's claims classified against your knowledge ledger. Claims appear in green (NEW), gray (KNOWN), or an intermediate state (EXTENDS). Claims slide up with a staggered 80ms animation.

✦ Cross-Article Connections

Two surfaces show how the current article connects to others:

  • Inline annotations: Below paragraphs that share claims with other articles, you'll see “Also in: [article title]” links (max 2 per paragraph). Based on cosine similarity ≥0.78 between claims.
  • ✦ CONNECTED READING section: At the bottom of the reader, up to 5 related articles with shared claim counts and “+ Queue” buttons (LIFO insertion — the queued article goes to the top of your queue).

✦ Related Articles

Below cross-article connections, three groups of related articles appear:

  • Same Topic: Other articles sharing the primary topic
  • Shared Concepts: Articles connected via embedding similarity — may not share an explicit topic but cover related ground
  • Same Source: Other articles from the same publication/domain

Max 3 per group, deduplicated across groups.

✦ Footer Bar

The persistent footer bar shows a Done button on the left and “UP NEXT” on the right, showing the title of the next queued article — tap to go directly to it. If the queue is empty, shows “← Back to feed” instead.

✦ Hierarchical Topic Feedback

After tapping Done, the PostReadInterestCard appears as a bottom sheet. It shows a tree display of topics: broad → specific → entity levels. Each level has +/− buttons for independent feedback. Smart expand: if there are ≤2 broad categories, they auto-expand so you see the specific topics immediately. Signals are level-specific — tapping + on a specific topic doesn't cascade up or down.

✦ Scroll-Aware Tracking

Reading progress is tracked by scroll depth, not just the “Done” button. If you scroll through 60% of an article, claims in the first 60% are marked as encountered in the knowledge ledger. Engagement level is determined by time: ‘read’ (>60s in the article) or ‘skim’ (≤60s). Tapping “Done” marks all claims regardless of scroll position.

✦ Micro-Delights

  • Pull-to-refresh: A rotating ✦ ornament (rubric-colored, 1200ms loop) replaces the default spinner
  • Claim reveal: Claims slide up with a staggered 80ms animation in the claims card
  • Completion flash: A gold (#c9a84c) sweep runs across the progress bar when you tap Done (600ms duration)

✦ Voice & AI

✦ AI Chat

Tap “Ask AI” from the … menu, or tap “Research more” from an entity popup. A bottom-sheet chat modal opens with the AI (Gemini Flash on the server) having full article context — title, summary, claims, topics, and truncated article text. You can ask follow-up questions, request explanations, or explore tangents. Supports threaded conversation. When opened from an entity popup, the question is pre-filled with entity context.

✦ Voice Feedback

Tap “Voice note” from the … menu while reading. The app starts recording locally. When you stop, the audio is uploaded to the server, transcribed via Soniox, and then Gemini extracts structured actions from the transcript — things like “research this topic”, “tag this article as X”, “remember that Y”. The extracted actions appear as tappable chips with status tracking.

The flow: Record locally → upload to server → Soniox transcription → Gemini action extraction → tappable action cards.

✦ Follow-Up Research Prompts

The pipeline generates 2–3 curiosity questions per article. The “✦ FURTHER INQUIRY” section appears at the end of the reader. Tap any question to spawn topic research via Gemini search grounding — finding real articles on the web and auto-ingesting them. The results appear in your feed after processing.

✦ Entity Research

The pipeline extracts 3–8 entities per article — people, books, concepts, companies, events, places, technologies. In the reader, entity mentions appear with dotted underlines.

✦ Entity Deep-Dive

Long-press an underlined entity name. A marginalia popup appears alongside the text with a synthesis of what the pipeline knows about that entity — a compact description drawn from the article context. At the bottom is a “Research more” button that opens the AI chat with the entity name pre-filled as a question, so you can dive deeper without losing your place.

✦ Knowledge System

This is the core of what makes Petrarca different from a plain read-later app. It tracks which claims you have encountered and how well you remember them, automatically in the background.

✦ How It Works

  1. Atomic decomposition: When an article enters the pipeline, Gemini Flash decomposes it into 10–30 atomic claims — single facts or assertions that stand alone.
  2. Embedding: Claims are embedded using Nomic-embed-text-v1.5 and stored in a vector index.
  3. Similarity: When you read an article, its claims are compared against all claims you've previously encountered. High similarity = “you know this.” Moderate similarity = “this extends what you know.”
  4. FSRS decay: Knowledge doesn't last forever. Each claim in your ledger has a stability value (in days). Over time, retrievability decays exponentially.
  5. Classification: Each claim is classified as NEW, KNOWN, or EXTENDS based on similarity scores and your current retrievability.

✦ Stability Values

Skim
9 days — article opened, scrolled
Read
30 days — article completed
Highlight
60 days — paragraph highlighted
Reinforcement
2.5× — re-encountering a claim multiplies its stability

✦ Cross-Article Connections

The knowledge index powers cross-article connections in the reader. When two articles share claims with cosine similarity ≥0.78, they're linked — surfaced as inline “Also in” annotations and in the “✦ CONNECTED READING” section at the bottom of the reader.

✦ Topic Normalization

Topics go through a canonical registry with LLM-verified merge-or-create for new topics. When the same concept appears under different names (“machine learning” vs “ML” vs “statistical learning”), the normalization step merges them. Automatic defragmentation runs when categories exceed limits, keeping the topic space clean.

✦ What You See

  • Paragraph dimming in Guided/New Only modes — familiar paragraphs fade
  • Novel paragraph markers in Guided/New Only modes — 2px green left border on novel content
  • Novelty hints in the feed — “3 new claims” tells you how much is genuinely new
  • Curiosity score affects feed ranking — articles with more novel claims float higher
  • Delta reports on the Topics tab — summaries of what's new in a topic area
  • Cross-article connections — inline “Also in” links and “✦ CONNECTED READING” section

✦ Interest Model

Separate from knowledge tracking, the interest model learns what topics you care about. Every interaction contributes a weighted signal: swiping to keep an article, opening it, finishing it, highlighting passages, and tapping topic chips all shape your profile. Signals decay over 30 days with Bayesian smoothing, so your interests can evolve naturally. The feed ranking formula uses this model as its primary input.

✦ The Pipeline

Understanding the processing pipeline helps you understand timing and quality. Every article passes through these stages on the server.

✦ Every 4 Hours on the Server

  1. Fetch bookmarks — Twitter bookmarks (twikit, GraphQL API, thread reconstruction) and Readwise Reader items (API).
  2. Fetch & clean — Content retrieved using multi-tier fallback (trafilatura, requests + browser headers). Markdown cleaned of nav menus, cookie banners, subscribe cruft, headings normalized.
  3. Structure — Gemini Flash produces: summary, one-line summary, sections, key claims, interest topics.
  4. Decompose — Article broken into 10–30 atomic claims, each typed as factual, procedural, evaluative, relational, or temporal.
  5. Extract entities — 3–8 entities per article: people, books, concepts, companies, events, places, technologies.
  6. Generate questions — 4 follow-up curiosity questions per article for the ✦ FURTHER INQUIRY section. A “More questions ✦” button generates 3 more on demand.
  7. Normalize topics — Topics checked against canonical registry with LLM-verified merge-or-create. Automatic defragmentation when categories exceed limits.
  8. Embed & index — All claims receive embeddings. Cosine similarity computed to build knowledge index: paragraph-level novelty mappings, cross-article novelty matrix, per-topic delta reports.
  9. Serve — Processed articles and knowledge index written to JSON and served via nginx on port 8083.

✦ When You Open the App

  1. App checks manifest hash against cached version
  2. If different: downloads new articles + knowledge index
  3. Merges with existing state (your signals, highlights, queue all preserved)
  4. Knowledge engine initializes, builds similarity lookup
  5. New articles appear in feed, ranked by interest + novelty

✦ Content Quality

Most articles process well, especially long-form journalism, blog posts, and technical writing. Some articles are low quality (very short tweets, paywalled stubs, donation pages). The clean_markdown step removes navigation, cookie banners, and subscribe cruft. Gemini Flash catches most garbage but not all. Wide net with some noise is the deliberate trade-off.

✦ Usage Patterns

The 30-Second Check

Open the app. Scan the feed. Swipe-dismiss anything stale. Swipe-queue anything promising. Close.

The 5-Minute Read

Tap an article. Use Guided mode — your eye goes straight to the new stuff (green-bordered paragraphs). Skim the dimmed paragraphs for context. Tap Done. Rate the topics.

The 15-Minute Deep Read

Open a queued article in Full mode. Read it all. Highlight interesting paragraphs. Long-press an interesting entity to learn more. When done, the interest card helps you tell the system what mattered. Follow topic links or connected reading to related articles.

The New-Only Speedrun

Switch to New Only mode. Only novel paragraphs are shown. Get the delta in 2 minutes. Great for articles in a domain you know well.

The Voice Note

While reading, open the … menu and tap “Voice note”. Record a quick thought. The audio uploads, gets transcribed by Soniox, and Gemini extracts actions: research topics, tags, things to remember. The extracted actions appear as tappable chips you can act on later.

The Desktop Reading Session

Open the web app. Use j/k to scan the feed. Press Enter to open an article. The 3-column reader shows metadata in the left margin, article center, and connections in the right margin. Use s to bookmark, a to ask AI, d when done. The next queued article loads automatically.

The Research Rabbit Hole

Long-press an underlined entity — a marginalia popup shows what the pipeline knows. Tap “Research more” — AI chat opens with context pre-filled. Then scroll to “✦ FURTHER INQUIRY” and tap a question. Gemini search finds real articles, auto-ingests them into your pipeline. Next time you open the app, they're waiting.

Exploring Your Knowledge Landscape

Open the ✦ drawer and tap “Your Landscape” to see a bubble map of all your topics — sized by article count, colored by activity trend. Check “Reading Trails” to see which threads of connected ideas you're following and where they bridge across topics.

✦ Non-Obvious Features

  1. Knowledge is automatic. Just reading an article updates your knowledge ledger. The FSRS decay handles forgetting naturally.
  2. Paragraph dimming is per-claim. A paragraph might contain 3 claims — 2 known, 1 new. The dimming reflects the aggregate, not a binary known/unknown.
  3. Swipe right = queue, swipe left = dismiss. Your thumb naturally reaches right for the deliberate action (save to queue), left for the common one (dismiss).
  4. Topic chips after reading shape your whole feed. The +/− signals on the post-read interest card have the highest weight (2.0). A single “+” meaningfully boosts related articles.
  5. The knowledge engine needs a knowledge index. Paragraph dimming and novelty scores only work after the pipeline generates a knowledge index with embeddings.
  6. Feed ranking is multi-factor. Interest model (60%) + curiosity score (40%) for the blended ranking. The interest model itself weighs interest match, freshness, discovery bonus, and variety.
  7. The web clipper captures selected text. Select a passage before clicking the clipper — that selection is saved as a highlight.
  8. Scroll position is saved. Every 2 seconds while reading, your position is saved. Leave and come back to exactly where you were.
  9. Continue Reading cards have progress bars. Progress is based on time spent relative to estimated read time, capped at 95%.
  10. All interactions are logged. Every tap, swipe, scroll, and mode change is logged to daily JSONL files.
  11. Entity underlines are contextual. Only entities the pipeline flagged as noteworthy get dotted underlines. Long-pressing opens a marginalia card with synthesis — not a generic Wikipedia lookup.
  12. Voice note actions are smart. After transcription, the LLM identifies intents. Actions appear as tappable chips with status tracking, not just raw transcript text.
  13. Cross-article connections use claim similarity. The “Also in” annotations and “✦ CONNECTED READING” section are based on cosine similarity ≥0.78 between claims, not just shared topics.
  14. The Activity Log shows everything. Pipeline runs, reading sessions, interest signals, research dispatches — all in a chronological timeline with filters.
  15. Related articles find connections three ways. Same topic, shared concepts (via embedding similarity), and same source. Three different lenses on “what to read next.”
  16. Queue auto-advance. After completing an article, if there are more in your queue, the reader auto-navigates to the next one with a toast notification. Press “← Feed” to escape.
  17. The ✦ drawer is your navigation hub. All secondary screens (Topics, Queue, Log, Landscape, Trails, Voice Notes, this Guide) are accessed through the drawer, not a tab bar.

✦ Known Issues

  • Low quality articles Swipe to dismiss. The pipeline casts a wide net.
  • Twitter cookies Cookie auth expires periodically. SSH to server, refresh cookies.
  • Raw markdown The markdown renderer handles most cases but edge cases exist.
  • No knowledge index If no knowledge index is loaded, dimming and novelty scores don't appear. The pipeline needs to generate the index.
  • No push notifs Rely on habit — open the app when you have reading time.
  • Offline limits Works offline with cached content but won't get new articles until it can reach the server.
  • Entity matching Pipeline matches entity names via string matching — may miss variant spellings or abbreviations.
  • Voice notes Recording is local-first, but upload + transcription need connectivity. Pending uploads retry automatically.
