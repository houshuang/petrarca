# Google's New AI Model Can Predict Protein Interactions Across Species

## AlphaFold 4 achieves 95% accuracy on cross-species protein binding prediction, opening doors for drug discovery and evolutionary biology

By **Rachel Kim** and **David Chen**, Technology Correspondents

Published March 5, 2026, 2:34 PM EST | Updated March 5, 2026, 5:12 PM EST

📖 6 min read

Google DeepMind unveiled AlphaFold 4 on Tuesday, a significant upgrade to the protein structure prediction system that won the 2024 Nobel Prize in Chemistry. The new version can predict how proteins from different species interact with each other — a capability that could transform drug development and our understanding of evolution.

"This is not an incremental improvement," said Demis Hassabis, CEO of Google DeepMind and Nobel laureate. "Cross-species protein interaction prediction was considered one of the hardest remaining problems in structural biology."

## What's New

AlphaFold 3, released in 2024, could predict the structure of individual proteins and simple protein complexes. AlphaFold 4 goes much further:

- **Cross-species binding**: Predict how a human antibody interacts with a bat coronavirus spike protein
- **Dynamic conformations**: Model multiple protein states, not just a single static structure
- **Drug-protein interactions**: Predict how candidate drug molecules bind to protein targets
- **Evolutionary analysis**: Map protein interaction networks across the tree of life

The system achieves 95.2% accuracy on a benchmark of known cross-species interactions, compared to 67% for the previous state of the art.

## The Science Behind It

AlphaFold 4 introduces a novel "evolutionary attention" mechanism that learns to align protein sequences across species. Rather than treating each protein in isolation, the model considers the entire evolutionary history of protein families.

"What makes this work is the insight that protein interactions are conserved across evolution," explained Dr. John Jumper, the lead scientist on the project. "A binding interface that works in a mouse usually works — with predictable modifications — in a human. AlphaFold 4 learns these patterns."

The training data includes:
- 200 million protein structures from the Protein Data Bank
- 2.3 billion protein sequences from UniRef
- 50,000 experimentally validated cross-species interactions
- Complete proteomes for 1,000 species

## Impact on Drug Discovery

Pharmaceutical companies are already lining up. Pfizer, Roche, and Novartis have signed early access agreements.

"Traditionally, testing a drug candidate against proteins from model organisms and then predicting its behavior in humans is a years-long process," said Dr. Sara Blakemore, head of computational biology at Pfizer. "AlphaFold 4 could compress that to days."

The implications for pandemic preparedness are particularly striking. During a novel virus outbreak, AlphaFold 4 could rapidly predict how viral proteins interact with human cell receptors, accelerating vaccine and therapeutic development.

## Criticism and Concerns

Not everyone is celebrating. Critics point out that DeepMind has not yet published the full model weights, limiting independent verification.

"The benchmark results are impressive, but we need to see the model in action on truly novel proteins," cautioned Dr. Mohammed Al-Rashid, a computational biologist at Cambridge. "There's a risk of overfitting to known protein families."

Others worry about dual-use risks — the same technology that predicts drug interactions could theoretically be used to engineer more dangerous pathogens.

Google DeepMind said the full paper will appear in Nature next week, and that a limited API will be available to academic researchers within 60 days.
