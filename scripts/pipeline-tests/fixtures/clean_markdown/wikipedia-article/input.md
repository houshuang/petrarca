# Byzantine Empire

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

For other uses, see [Byzantine Empire (disambiguation)](/wiki/Byzantine_Empire_(disambiguation)).

The **Byzantine Empire**, also referred to as the **Eastern Roman Empire**, was the continuation of the [](/wiki/Roman_Empire) in its eastern provinces during [Late Antiquity](/wiki/Late_Antiquity) and the [Middle Ages](/wiki/Middle_Ages), when its capital city was [Constantinople](/wiki/Constantinople).[1][2] It survived the fragmentation and fall of the [Western Roman Empire](/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire) in the 5th century AD and continued to exist for an additional thousand years until the fall of [Constantinople](/wiki/Constantinople) to the [Ottoman Turks](/wiki/Ottoman_Turks) in 1453.[3]

## History [edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Byzantine_Empire&action=edit&section=1)

### Origins [[edit]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Byzantine_Empire&action=edit&section=2)

The Byzantine Empire was a vast and powerful civilization with origins that can be traced to 330 A.D., when the Roman emperor [](/wiki/Constantine_I)[Constantine I](/wiki/Constantine_I) dedicated a "New Rome" on the site of the ancient Greek colony of Byzantium.[4][5]

Though the western half of the Roman Empire crumbled and fell in 476 A.D., the eastern half survived for 1,000 more years, spawning a rich tradition of art, literature and learning and serving as a military buffer between Europe and Asia.[6]

### Justinian I [[edit]]

[](/wiki/Justinian_I) reigned from 527 to 565 and was known for his ambitious building program, including the construction of the [Hagia Sophia](/wiki/Hagia_Sophia).[7] Under Justinian, the empire reached its greatest territorial extent, reconquering many of the formerly Roman lands around the Mediterranean basin.[8][9]

Justinian also reformed the legal system through the [Corpus Juris Civilis](/wiki/Corpus_Juris_Civilis), a codification of Roman law that would influence European legal tradition for centuries.[citation needed]

### Iconoclasm [edit]

The [Byzantine Iconoclasm](/wiki/Byzantine_Iconoclasm) refers to two periods in the history of the Byzantine Empire when the use of religious images (icons) was opposed by religious and imperial authorities.[10][11][12]

## Culture [[edit]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Byzantine_Empire&action=edit&section=5)

Byzantine culture was marked by a synthesis of Greco-Roman and Christian elements. The empire preserved and transmitted classical Greek literature and Roman law to the Renaissance world.

### Art and Architecture [edit]

Byzantine art is characterized by its use of gold backgrounds, hieratic compositions, and iconographic traditions. The construction of [Hagia Sophia](/wiki/Hagia_Sophia) in 537 remains one of the greatest architectural achievements in history.[13]

### Literature [[edit]]

Byzantine literature comprises texts written in medieval [](/wiki/Greek_language) from the time of the fall of Rome to the fall of Constantinople.[14] It was heavily influenced by classical Greek models and Christian theological traditions.
